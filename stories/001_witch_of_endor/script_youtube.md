# Story 001: The Witch of Endor

### Scripture: 1 Samuel 28 (KJV primary, ESV cross-reference)

## [SCENE 01] — HOOK / COLD OPEN
**[00:00–00:45] | Duration: ~45 sec**

> *[MUSIC: Low drone, distant war drums. Fade in.]*

There is a moment — recorded in the oldest books of civilization — where a king of Israel, the anointed of God, sat in the dark and trembled.

Not from cold. Not from cowardice.

From silence.

God had stopped speaking to him. And in that silence, a desperate king did something so forbidden, so strange, that scholars have debated it for three thousand years.

He called up the dead.

This is the true story of Saul, the Witch of Endor, and the ghost of the prophet Samuel — one of the most haunting nights in all of Scripture.

---

## [SCENE 02] — CONTEXT: SAUL'S KINGDOM UNRAVELING
**[00:45–02:00] | Duration: ~75 sec**

> *[MUSIC: Somber strings, sparse. Slow build.]*

To understand what brought Saul to a witch's door, we have to understand how far he had fallen.

Saul was Israel's first king — tall, striking, chosen by God through the prophet Samuel. He began with promise. But over the years, he disobeyed God on two decisive occasions that cost him everything.

First, he offered an unauthorized sacrifice before battle, taking priestly authority that wasn't his. Then — and this was the final fracture — he failed to carry out God's command to utterly destroy the Amalekites. He spared their king, Agag, and kept the best livestock for himself.

Samuel confronted him with words that became one of Scripture's most haunting lines: *"To obey is better than sacrifice, and to hearken than the fat of rams."* (1 Samuel 15:22, KJV)

God had rejected Saul as king. And then — Samuel died.

Samuel was more than a prophet to Saul. He was the last direct line. The voice of God in human form. The man who had anointed him, corrected him, interceded for him. When Samuel died, something in Saul's world went dark.

Israel mourned. Saul mourned. And in a moment of legal clarity that may have been too little too late, Saul did do one thing right: he expelled all the mediums and necromancers from the land, as the Law of Moses required. (1 Samuel 28:3)

But that law — and that expulsion — would become bitterly ironic before the night was over.

---

## [SCENE 03] — THE PHILISTINE THREAT
**[02:00–03:15] | Duration: ~75 sec**

> *[MUSIC: Rising tension. Low brass. Distant percussion.]*

Now the Philistines gathered their armies.

Not a raiding party. Not a skirmish. A full military mobilization, encamped at Shunem in the Jezreel Valley — a wide, flat killing ground that favored their superior iron weaponry and war chariots.

Saul assembled Israel at Gilboa. He looked across that valley and felt it in his chest: dread. Not the sharp fear of battle — he had lived with that his whole life. This was different. The text says he was *"sore afraid, and his heart greatly trembled."* (1 Samuel 28:5, KJV)

The ESV renders it: *"his heart trembled greatly."*

Something had broken in him. He was staring at an army he wasn't sure he could defeat, fighting for a kingdom he wasn't sure God still wanted him to have.

So he did what any desperate man of faith does first: he prayed.

---

## [SCENE 04] — THE SILENCE OF GOD
**[03:15–04:00] | Duration: ~45 sec**

> *[MUSIC: Near silence. A single mournful string note.]*

*"And when Saul enquired of the LORD, the LORD answered him not, neither by dreams, nor by Urim, nor by prophets."* (1 Samuel 28:6, KJV)

There it is. The most terrifying verse in this whole story — and it says nothing spectacular. It is simply silence.

God spoke to his people through three channels: prophetic dreams, the Urim and Thummim — sacred lots kept by the High Priest — and through the prophets themselves.

Saul tried all three.

Nothing.

The heaven above him was brass. And standing on the eve of the most important battle of his life, Saul made a decision that sealed his fate.

---

## [SCENE 05] — THE COMMAND TO FIND A MEDIUM
**[04:00–04:45] | Duration: ~45 sec**

> *[MUSIC: Unsettling minor key. Subtle dissonance.]*

*"Then said Saul unto his servants, Seek me a woman that hath a familiar spirit, that I may go to her, and enquire of her."* (1 Samuel 28:7, KJV)

Think about what is happening here. The king who expelled all the mediums from the land is now asking his servants to find him one. He knows this is forbidden. Leviticus 19:31 calls it defilement. Leviticus 20:27 calls it a capital offense.

Saul knows these laws. He wrote the enforcement orders himself.

And yet.

His servants told him: there is a woman at Endor.

---

## [SCENE 06] — THE DISGUISE AND THE JOURNEY
**[04:45–05:45] | Duration: ~60 sec**

> *[MUSIC: Low, nocturnal ambience. Wind. Sparse strings.]*

Saul disguised himself.

Put on common clothes — not the robes of a king, not the armor of a commander. Just a man. Anonymous. Desperate. He took two servants and they traveled in the dark, because what he was about to do could not be done in daylight.

Endor lay on the other side of the Philistine encampment. To reach it, Saul had to slip through, or around, enemy lines — the king of Israel, alone, in the dark, breaking his own law to find a woman he had been trying to run out of the country.

The geography here is almost deliberately humiliating. The man who should be inside a war tent, surrounded by generals and priests, is instead crawling through the dark toward a witch's door.

He arrives. He knocks.

*"Divine unto me by the familiar spirit, and bring me him up, whom I shall name unto thee."* (1 Samuel 28:8, KJV)

---

## [SCENE 07] — THE WOMAN'S FEAR AND SAUL'S OATH
**[05:45–06:30] | Duration: ~45 sec**

> *[MUSIC: Still. Almost no music. Raw.]*

The woman is afraid.

She knows what Saul did to practitioners like her. She says it plainly: *"Behold, thou knowest what Saul hath done, how he hath cut off those that have familiar spirits, and the wizards, out of the land: wherefore then layest thou a snare for my life, to cause me to die?"* (1 Samuel 28:9, KJV)

She thinks this is a trap. A royal sting operation.

Saul swears to her by God — by the very God who is no longer answering him — that she will face no punishment. It is one of the darkest ironies in the chapter: invoking God's name to obtain something God explicitly forbids.

She agrees. She asks: who do you want me to bring up?

*"Bring me up Samuel."*

---

## [SCENE 08] — THE APPARITION
**[06:30–07:30] | Duration: ~60 sec**

> *[MUSIC: Sudden shift. Deep, resonant, unsettling. Not horror — sacred dread.]*

What happens next is one of the most debated passages in all of biblical literature.

When the woman performs her ritual — she screams.

Not a performance. A genuine, involuntary cry of terror.

*"And when the woman saw Samuel, she cried with a loud voice."* (1 Samuel 28:12, KJV)

Something appeared that was not what she was accustomed to conjuring. Whatever she normally produced — illusion, deception, demonic counterfeit — this was different. This frightened her. She immediately turns to Saul: *"Why hast thou deceived me? For thou art Saul."*

His disguise is undone. And in the moment of recognition, there is Samuel.

She describes him: an old man, covered with a mantle. And Saul knew him. He bowed his face to the ground and prostrated himself.

---

## [SCENE 09] — SAMUEL SPEAKS
**[07:30–09:00] | Duration: ~90 sec**

> *[MUSIC: Complete silence under narration. Let the words breathe.]*

Samuel's first words are not comforting.

*"Why hast thou disquieted me, to bring me up?"* (1 Samuel 28:15, KJV)

There is something devastating in that question. The word "disquieted" — to disturb someone's rest. The prophet who spent his life being interrupted for God's work, now disturbed even in death by the man he spent his ministry trying to correct.

Saul pours out his desperation: the Philistines are here, God won't answer, I have no one else. I need to know what to do.

Samuel's response is one of the most unflinching speeches in the Bible. No comfort. No final mercy. Just truth.

He reminds Saul exactly why this is happening: God has torn the kingdom from him and given it to David — *just as Samuel said he would.* This was not a surprise. The verdict was already delivered, years ago.

And then the prophecy: *"Moreover the LORD will also deliver Israel with thee into the hand of the Philistines: and to morrow shalt thou and thy sons be with me."* (1 Samuel 28:19, KJV)

Tomorrow.

Tomorrow, Saul would be dead. His sons would be dead. Israel would fall.

The word *"with me"* — with Samuel, in death — is one of the most discussed phrases in Hebrew eschatology. It implies Saul's soul would go to the same place as Samuel's. The medieval rabbis and Christian theologians have argued about it for centuries.

But for Saul, in that moment, it meant only one thing.

His time was up.

---

## [SCENE 10] — SAUL'S COLLAPSE
**[09:00–09:45] | Duration: ~45 sec**

> *[MUSIC: Quiet, low swell. Not dramatic. Grief.]*

Saul fell immediately. Full length upon the ground. The great king of Israel, on the dirt floor of a medium's house, unable to move.

He hadn't eaten all day. Fear had emptied him. The pronouncement had finished the job.

The woman — the witch, the outcast, the woman whose profession he had made illegal — knelt beside him.

She begged him to eat. He refused. His servants joined her plea. Finally, he rose and sat upon a bed.

She killed the fatted calf. She baked unleavened bread. She set food before the king who had come to destroy everything she was.

And before that long terrible night was over, Saul and his servants ate. Then they rose up and went away in the dark.

---

## [SCENE 11] — CLOSING REFLECTION
**[09:45–11:00] | Duration: ~75 sec**

> *[MUSIC: Slowly resolving. Somber but not hopeless.]*

The next day, the battle of Gilboa. Jonathan, Abinadab, and Malchishua — Saul's sons — died. Saul himself, wounded by archers, fell on his own sword rather than be taken by the Philistines. (1 Samuel 31)

Everything Samuel said came true. Before the sun went down the following day, it was finished.

What do we make of this story?

Theologians still argue about the nature of what appeared at Endor. Was it truly Samuel's spirit, summoned against the order of creation? A demon masquerading in Samuel's form? A vision permitted by God as one final act of judgment — a last word delivered to a man who had stopped listening to the living?

The text doesn't fully resolve it. It simply records what happened, and what was said, and what came true.

But here is what cannot be debated: Saul's tragedy was not that he was evil. It was that he was almost obedient. Almost faithful. He obeyed God right up to the point where obedience was costly. And in those gaps — in those moments of *almost* — everything unraveled.

The witch of Endor is a strange figure of unexpected mercy in a bleak story. She showed more compassion to her enemy king than almost anyone else in his court.

And Samuel — even dead — told the truth.
